# LinkedIn — How it actually works

Before you update anything, it helps to understand what LinkedIn's search algorithm is doing and what a recruiter actually sees. Most people write their profiles for humans and forget the algorithm decides who gets surfaced first.

---

## How recruiter search works

Recruiters don't scroll through profiles. They run a filtered search: job title, location, experience level, sometimes specific skills. LinkedIn's algorithm ranks results based on how closely your profile matches the query — and the weighting goes like this:

**Headline > Skills section > Job titles > About section > Bullet points**

That means a keyword that lives only in your About section barely moves the needle. The same keyword in your headline and pinned top-5 skills makes you show up in far more searches.

Practical takeaway: if a recruiter is searching "RAG engineer" or "LLM engineer", that phrase needs to be in your headline and your top skills — not just buried in a bullet from a job three months ago.

---

## What a recruiter looks at in the first 8 seconds

When a recruiter clicks on a profile, their eyes go to:
1. Your headline (does this person match what I'm hiring for?)
2. Your location (is relocation needed?)
3. Your top 3 pinned skills (quick shorthand for specialisation)
4. Your most recent job title and company
5. The first 2 lines of your most recent experience bullets

If those five things don't immediately match the role, they're back to search results. The About section, certifications, and projects only get read if they've already decided to keep going.

## Open to Work — recruiter-only mode

Go to your profile → "Open to" → "Finding a new job". You'll see two options:
- **All LinkedIn members** — shows a green #OpenToWork frame on your profile photo (visible to everyone including your current employer)
- **Recruiters only** — signals to recruiters running talent searches without the public frame

If you're job hunting quietly, use "Recruiters only". It hides the frame but still puts you in recruiter search results for active candidates.

Set the roles you're open to ("AI Engineer", "Software Engineer", "Machine Learning Engineer"), your preferred locations, and when you can start.

---

## Job alerts

Go to a job search → filter by role + location → click "Set alert". LinkedIn emails you new matching listings daily or weekly depending on your setting.

Most useful filters to set:
- Job title: "AI Engineer", "Machine Learning Engineer", "Software Engineer"
- Location: Chennai, Bangalore, Hyderabad, Mumbai, Pune + Remote
- Experience level: Entry level, Associate
- Date posted: Last 24 hours (catches freshly posted roles before they fill)

You can also follow specific companies and get notified when they post anything.

## Easy Apply vs. Apply on company website

**Easy Apply** (the LinkedIn button): your application stays within LinkedIn, you submit your LinkedIn profile and optionally a resume. Fast, but the recruiter sees hundreds of these. Your profile needs to stand out on its own.

**Apply on company website**: redirects you out. Slower, but usually fewer applicants and you can tailor your resume to the specific role before submitting.

When to use which:
- If the company is small-to-mid size and the role is specific, apply on the website and tailor the resume.
- If it's a volume play (lots of similar companies, lots of similar roles), Easy Apply is fine as long as your LinkedIn profile is strong enough to speak for itself.

## Responding to recruiter InMail and connection requests

Recruiters reach you two ways: a connection request (often with a message) or an InMail (direct message without needing to be connected first).

**What to look for before responding:**
- Is the role actually relevant? (Don't waste time on something totally off)
- Is it a real company with a real recruiter name, or a scraped message from a bot?
- Does the message mention the actual role title and company, or is it vague?

**How to reply to a relevant one:**
```
Hi [Name],

Thanks for reaching out. I'm open to hearing more — could you share the role 
description and the company name? I'd like to make sure it's a good fit before 
we set up a call.

Best,
Omprakash
```

Keep it short, professional, and put the ball back in their court to share specifics. If they can't tell you the company name (some recruiters work on confidential searches), that's fine — ask for the JD at minimum.

**If a recruiter asks you to apply through a link before any conversation:**
That's a volume recruiter filling a pipeline, not someone advocating for you. It's still worth applying if the role fits, but manage expectations.

**If a recruiter schedules a call:**
Have two things ready: a 2-minute version of your background (the story from college → research → PTC → what you're looking for next), and 2-3 questions about the role and team. The call is a filter for them and an evaluation for you.
